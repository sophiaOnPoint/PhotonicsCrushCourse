1 · Introduction

A waveguide is a structure that confines and guides electromagnetic waves. The simplest model in integrated photonics is the slab waveguide: an infinite planar layer of high-index material sandwiched between two semi-infinite layers of lower-index material.

Despite its apparent simplicity, the slab waveguide captures almost every phenomenon that matters in real devices — mode quantization, the concept of an effective index, mode cut-off, and the tradeoff between confinement and field penetration into the cladding. It is the foundation for understanding ridge waveguides, optical fibers, and photonic crystal slabs.

Why does light stay in the core?

The mechanism is total internal reflection (TIR). When a ray inside a high-index medium strikes a low-index interface at a shallow enough angle (measured from the surface), it is reflected with 100 % efficiency — it cannot escape. The critical angle $\theta_c$ is found from Snell's law at the point where the refracted ray grazes the interface:

Snell → Critical Angle
$$n_\text{core}\sin\theta_c = n_\text{clad}\sin 90° \implies \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{n_\text{clad}}{n_\text{core}}\right)$$

Rays that hit the interface at angles larger than $\theta_c$ (measured from the normal) are totally reflected and remain trapped. The wave-optics version of this argument leads to the same conclusion: guided modes exist only when $n_\text{clad} < n_\text{eff} < n_\text{core}$, where $n_\text{eff} = \beta / k_0$ is the effective index of the mode.

💡 Slab waveguides appear everywhere in photonics: silicon-on-insulator (SOI) platforms, III–V laser slabs, thin-film lithium niobate, and polymer waveguides all start from this same 3-layer geometry.

2 · Geometry & Index Profile

We define coordinates so that:

  • $x$ is the transverse direction (across the slab thickness).
  • $z$ is the propagation direction (along the waveguide).
  • $y$ is the lateral direction — the structure is infinite and uniform in $y$.

The slab has total thickness $d = 2a$ and is centred at $x = 0$. The refractive index profile is a step function:

Index Profile
$$n(x) = \begin{cases} n_\text{core} & |x| < a \\ n_\text{clad} & |x| > a \end{cases}$$

3 · Maxwell's Equations

Everything starts from Maxwell's macroscopic curl equations in a linear, isotropic, non-magnetic, source-free medium:

Maxwell curl equations (time domain)
$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\mu_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \varepsilon_0 n^2 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

We assume a time-harmonic (monochromatic) field with angular frequency $\omega$ and use the phasor convention $e^{-i\omega t}$, so $\partial / \partial t \to -i\omega$. The curl equations become:

Time-harmonic Maxwell (frequency domain)
$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = i\omega\mu_0 \mathbf{H}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{H} = -i\omega\varepsilon_0 n^2 \mathbf{E}$$

We also need the divergence conditions. In a region of uniform index, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0$ (no free charges and $n$ is constant).

From curl-curl to the vector wave equation

Take the curl of the first equation and substitute the second:

Derivation
$$\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = i\omega\mu_0(\nabla\times\mathbf{H}) = i\omega\mu_0(-i\omega\varepsilon_0 n^2)\mathbf{E} = \omega^2\mu_0\varepsilon_0 n^2\mathbf{E}$$

Apply the vector identity $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E}$ and use $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}=0$:

Vector Helmholtz Equation
$$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} + k_0^2\, n^2\, \mathbf{E} = 0, \qquad k_0 = \frac{\omega}{c} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$$

where $k_0$ is the free-space wavenumber and $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$ is the speed of light. Each Cartesian component of $\mathbf{E}$ satisfies this scalar Helmholtz equation in a region where $n$ is constant.

4 · Mode Ansatz & the 1D Eigenvalue Problem

Because the structure is uniform in $y$ and $z$, we look for solutions of the form:

Guided-mode ansatz
$$\mathbf{E}(x,y,z,t) = \mathbf{E}(x)\, e^{i\beta z}\, e^{-i\omega t}$$

where $\beta$ is the (unknown) propagation constant along $z$. Substituting into the scalar Helmholtz equation and using $\partial/\partial y=0$, $\partial/\partial z = i\beta$:

Substitution
$$\frac{d^2 E}{dx^2} + (k_0^2 n^2 - \beta^2) E = 0$$

This is a 1D eigenvalue problem. Define the effective index $n_\text{eff} = \beta / k_0$ (the phase index experienced by the mode). Then:

Transverse equation in each region
$$\frac{d^2 E}{dx^2} + k_0^2(n^2 - n_\text{eff}^2)\, E = 0$$

The sign of $(n^2 - n_\text{eff}^2)$ determines the character of the solution in each layer:

Core  ($|x|<a$,  $n=n_\text{core}$)

If $n_\text{eff} < n_\text{core}$, then $n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{eff}^2 > 0$, so the solution oscillates with transverse wavenumber:

$$\kappa = k_0\sqrt{n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{eff}^2}$$

Cladding  ($|x|>a$,  $n=n_\text{clad}$)

If $n_\text{eff} > n_\text{clad}$, then $n_\text{clad}^2 - n_\text{eff}^2 < 0$, so the solution decays exponentially with decay constant:

$$\gamma = k_0\sqrt{n_\text{eff}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2}$$
🔑 Guided modes exist only when $n_\text{clad} < n_\text{eff} < n_\text{core}$. This ensures oscillation in the core and decay in the cladding — exactly the TIR picture recast in wave-optics language.

5 · TE vs TM Modes

In a planar slab, modes separate cleanly into two polarisation families depending on which field component is transverse to the propagation plane ($x$–$z$).

TE — Transverse Electric

Only $E_y \neq 0$ among the electric field components. The non-zero field components are:

$E_y$, $H_x$, $H_z$

$H_x$ and $H_z$ are derived from $E_y$ via Maxwell's curl equation.

TM — Transverse Magnetic

Only $H_y \neq 0$ among the magnetic field components. The non-zero field components are:

$H_y$, $E_x$, $E_z$

TM modes are generally less well-confined than TE modes at the same order (see §11).

TE field components from Maxwell

For TE modes, $E_y(x) e^{i\beta z}$ is the only independent field. The magnetic field components follow directly from Faraday's law $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = i\omega\mu_0\mathbf{H}$:

TE magnetic fields
$$H_x = -\frac{\beta}{\omega\mu_0} E_y, \qquad H_z = \frac{i}{\omega\mu_0}\frac{dE_y}{dx}$$

So we only need to solve for $E_y(x)$. All other fields follow.

6 · Field Solutions in Each Region

In the core ($|x| < a$) the transverse equation is $d^2E_y/dx^2 + \kappa^2 E_y = 0$, which has sinusoidal solutions. Because the slab is symmetric, modes split into even (cosine) and odd (sine) families:

Core field — even modes (TE₀, TE₂, …)
$$E_y(x) = A\cos(\kappa x), \quad |x| \le a$$
Core field — odd modes (TE₁, TE₃, …)
$$E_y(x) = A\sin(\kappa x), \quad |x| \le a$$

In the cladding ($|x| > a$) the equation is $d^2E_y/dx^2 - \gamma^2 E_y = 0$. We keep only the decaying solution (the growing solution diverges and is unphysical):

Cladding field ($x > a$)
$$E_y(x) = B\, e^{-\gamma(x - a)}, \quad x > a$$

By symmetry, for $x < -a$: $E_y(-x) = \pm E_y(x)$ (plus for even, minus for odd). The amplitude $B$ is determined by the boundary conditions at $x = a$.

Figure 2 — Qualitative field profiles for the first three TE modes

Notice that mode number $m$ has exactly $m$ nodes (zeros) in the core. Higher-order modes have more oscillations and, at the same frequency, are less tightly confined — their effective index is closer to $n_\text{clad}$.

7 · Boundary Conditions

At the core–cladding interface $x = a$, the tangential electric and magnetic fields must be continuous. For TE modes the tangential fields are $E_y$ and $H_z$ (which is proportional to $dE_y/dx$):

  1. Continuity of $E_y$ at $x=a$: core value equals cladding value.
  2. Continuity of $H_z \propto dE_y/dx$ at $x=a$: slope must also match.

For the even TE mode with core field $A\cos(\kappa x)$ and cladding field $Be^{-\gamma(x-a)}$:

Matching $E_y$ at $x = a$
$$A\cos(\kappa a) = B$$
Matching $dE_y/dx$ at $x = a$
$$-A\kappa\sin(\kappa a) = -B\gamma$$

Divide the second equation by the first (eliminating the amplitude $A$):

Even TE dispersion relation (un-normalised)
$$\kappa \tan(\kappa a) = \gamma$$

Repeating for the odd mode (core field $A\sin(\kappa x)$):

Odd TE dispersion relation (un-normalised)
$$-\kappa \cot(\kappa a) = \gamma$$

These two equations, combined with the constraint $\kappa^2 + \gamma^2 = k_0^2(n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2)$, determine the allowed values of $\beta$ (and hence $n_\text{eff}$) for each guided mode.

8 · The Normalised Dispersion Relation

Introduce three dimensionless variables that absorb the physical parameters:

Normalised variables
$$u = \kappa a = k_0 a\sqrt{n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{eff}^2}, \quad w = \gamma a = k_0 a\sqrt{n_\text{eff}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2}$$ $$V = k_0 a\sqrt{n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad u^2 + w^2 = V^2$$

$V$ is the normalised frequency (or V-number). It depends only on the wavelength, index contrast, and thickness — not on $\beta$. The dispersion relations become:

Even TE modes
$$u\tan u = w, \qquad u^2 + w^2 = V^2$$
Odd TE modes
$$-u\cot u = w, \qquad u^2 + w^2 = V^2$$

These are transcendental equations that must be solved numerically (or graphically). The graphical solution is one of the most instructive tools in waveguide theory: plot $u\tan u$ (even branches) and $-u\cot u$ (odd branches) together with the circle $w = \sqrt{V^2 - u^2}$ of radius $V$. Each intersection is a guided mode.

10 · V Number & Mode Counting

The V-number (also called the normalised frequency or V parameter) encapsulates all the geometry and material information that determines how many guided modes exist:

V number — two equivalent forms
$$V = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\cdot\frac{d}{2}\cdot\sqrt{n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2} = \frac{\pi d}{\lambda}\,\text{NA}$$

where $\text{NA} = \sqrt{n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2}$ is the numerical aperture of the waveguide (related to the acceptance angle in free space).

Cutoff conditions

Mode TE$_m$ ($m = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$) exists as a guided mode only if $V > m\pi/2$, i.e., at cutoff its V-number equals exactly $m\pi/2$. The number of guided TE modes is therefore:

Number of guided TE modes
$$M = \left\lfloor \frac{V}{\pi/2} \right\rfloor + 1 = \left\lfloor \frac{2V}{\pi} \right\rfloor + 1$$
$V$ rangeGuided TE modesSingle-mode?
$0 < V < \pi/2 \approx 1.571$TE₀ only✓ Yes
$\pi/2 \le V < \pi \approx 3.14$TE₀, TE₁✗ No
$\pi \le V < 3\pi/2 \approx 4.71$TE₀, TE₁, TE₂✗ No
$3\pi/2 \le V < 2\pi \approx 6.28$TE₀, TE₁, TE₂, TE₃✗ No

Single-mode condition

The waveguide supports only the fundamental TE₀ mode when $V < \pi/2$. Solving for thickness:

Single-mode thickness condition
$$d < \frac{\lambda}{2\,\text{NA}} = \frac{\lambda}{2\sqrt{n_\text{core}^2 - n_\text{clad}^2}}$$

For a silicon slab ($n_\text{core}=3.48$, $n_\text{clad}=1.44$) at $\lambda = 1.55\,\mu\text{m}$: $\text{NA} \approx 3.17$, so single-mode requires $d < 0.244\,\mu\text{m}$.

Note: The single-mode condition above is for TE modes only. TM₀ also exists at all V. A truly single-mode waveguide (supporting only one polarization and one mode) requires the slab geometry to be abandoned in favour of a fully 2D-confined ridge or wire waveguide.

11 · TM Modes

For TM modes, the magnetic field $H_y(x)e^{i\beta z}$ plays the role of the scalar field. The transverse equation is the same: $d^2 H_y / dx^2 + (k_0^2 n^2 - \beta^2) H_y = 0$. However, the boundary condition at the interface is different: the normal component of $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 n^2 \mathbf{E}$ must be continuous, which for $E_x \propto (1/n^2) dH_y/dx$ means that $(1/n^2)\,dH_y/dx$ is continuous.

Applying the TM boundary conditions at $x = a$ gives:

Even TM dispersion relation
$$u\tan u = \left(\frac{n_\text{core}}{n_\text{clad}}\right)^2 w$$
Odd TM dispersion relation
$$-u\cot u = \left(\frac{n_\text{core}}{n_\text{clad}}\right)^2 w$$

The factor $(n_\text{core}/n_\text{clad})^2 > 1$ makes the right-hand side larger, which pushes the graphical intersection closer to the $u$-axis — meaning the TM$_m$ effective index is lower than TE$_m$ for the same parameters. In a strongly asymmetric guide, TM modes are sometimes not supported at all.

The cutoff V-values for TM modes are the same as for TE modes (both at $V = m\pi/2$), but the confinement is weaker at any given V.

⚡ In silicon photonics (high index contrast), the TE–TM splitting in effective index can be large (0.3–0.5 neff units), which is why many silicon devices are designed specifically for one polarization.

12 · Confinement Factor

The confinement factor $\Gamma$ measures what fraction of the guided mode's energy is located inside the core:

Confinement factor (TE mode)
$$\Gamma = \frac{\displaystyle\int_{-a}^{a} |E_y|^2\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |E_y|^2\,dx}$$

For the even TE modes, integrating the piecewise field analytically:

Analytical confinement factor (even TE modes)
$$\Gamma = \frac{1 + \dfrac{\sin 2u}{2u}}{1 + \dfrac{\sin 2u}{2u} + \dfrac{\cos^2 u}{w}}$$

where $u$ and $w$ are the normalised transverse wavenumbers from the dispersion solution. As $V \to \infty$ (very thick slab or short wavelength), $u \to m\pi/2$ and $w \to \infty$, so $\Gamma \to 1$. At cutoff ($w = 0$), $\Gamma \to 0$.

$\Gamma$ appears directly in the gain coefficient for a laser: the material gain $g_\text{mat}$ produces a modal gain $\Gamma g_\text{mat}$, so a poorly confined mode (low $\Gamma$) sees less gain from the active core.

In the interactive explorer above (§9), the confinement factor for each mode can be read from the mode table — try reducing the slab thickness to watch $\Gamma$ drop towards zero as the mode approaches cutoff.

13 · Numerical Methods

When the analytical approach is not available (e.g., graded-index profiles, multi-layer slabs, or full 2D cross-sections), we discretise the transverse equation using the finite-difference method.

Finite-difference discretisation

Approximate the second derivative with a central difference on a uniform grid with spacing $\Delta x$:

Second-order central difference
$$\frac{d^2 E_i}{dx^2} \approx \frac{E_{i+1} - 2E_i + E_{i-1}}{\Delta x^2}$$

Substituting into $d^2E/dx^2 = (\beta^2 - k_0^2 n_i^2)E$:

Matrix eigenvalue problem
$$\mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{E} = \beta^2\,\mathbf{E}, \qquad A_{ii} = -\frac{2}{\Delta x^2} + k_0^2 n_i^2, \quad A_{i,i\pm 1} = \frac{1}{\Delta x^2}$$

Solving for the largest eigenvalue $\beta^2$ gives the fundamental mode, and the corresponding eigenvector is the field profile $E_y(x_i)$.

Convergence

Because the central difference is second-order accurate ($O(\Delta x^2)$), the error in $n_\text{eff}$ scales as $\Delta x^2$. Halving $\Delta x$ reduces the error by a factor of 4. This is confirmed by the convergence plot below (produced by the companion Python scripts):
